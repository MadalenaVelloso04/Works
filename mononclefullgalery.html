Personal project 2023

Project Overview

In designing the posters for Mon Oncle, I chose to center the compositions around the character of Monsieur Hulot and his signature elements, reflecting the unique routine that defines his quirky presence in the film. The focus was on recurring details—his hat, pipe, raincoat, and distinct stance—that subtly speak to his personality and lifestyle. These everyday items carry a sense of rhythm and continuity in Mon Oncle, becoming symbols of Hulot's resistance to the impersonal modern world around him. By isolating these familiar elements, I wanted to convey how Hulot's character exudes warmth and individuality in a landscape increasingly driven by conformity.

Concept and Creation

Inspired by the Bauhaus and similar movements that emphasize simplicity and functionality, I took a minimalist approach to highlight these key elements. Textures in the posters hint at the feel of Hulot's world—a mix of rough, warm textures against sleek, flat surfaces symbolizing the contrast between his cozy, lived-in space and the sterile modern environment he often finds himself in. The stripped-down compositions allowed me to draw attention to Hulot's objects without distraction, reinforcing the daily rituals and interactions that Tati's film subtly celebrates. This focus also reflects the essence of Hulot's world: an organic, playful rebellion against an increasingly uniform society.

The colors maintain a bright, cheerful palette, capturing the film's lighthearted tone. Mon Oncle has a gentle humor and charm that I wanted to preserve by using fresh, inviting hues. This brightness complements the character's whimsical defiance of modernity, which feels optimistic rather than cynical. Through these posters, I hoped to honor Jacques Tati's iconic style—his ability to criticize without losing humor, to dissect routine without stripping it of joy—by blending color, minimalism, and texture in homage to Hulot's enduring charm.
